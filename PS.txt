Production Sheduling: - 
	Production scheduling is a time-based planning process in manufacturing where all production-related activities are organized, sequenced,
	and allocated over a specific period.
	
	It ensures that:
		All manufacturing activities (procurement of inputs, labor planning, investments, logistics, etc.) are planned in advance
		Resources are mapped to each stage of production, clarifying:
			What resources are needed
			When they are needed
			At which production stage they are consumed
			A sequential, time-bound schedule is created so production flows smoothly without delays
			The organization avoids resource shortages during production by estimating requirements beforehand
	
	Production Planning?
	
	Production Planning is a strategic, upfront decision-making process that manufacturers carry out before actual production begins.
	It focuses on what to produce, how much to produce, and whether the organization is capable of producing it efficiently and sustainably.
	It acts as the foundation of the entire production cycle, on top of which production scheduling and execution are built.

	Production planning typically includes:
		Finalizing the product to be manufactured
		Deciding the production volume
		Performing capacity planning
		Identifying materials and resources required
		Defining broad production timelines
	The goal is to maximize efficiency, reduce costs, ensure quality, and maintain a sustainable production system.

	Key Steps : - 
	1. Analyzing Customer Demand

		Production exists only because of demand
		If demand exceeds production → orders remain unfulfilled
		If production exceeds demand → inventory and cost issues
		Therefore, determining accurate market demand is the starting point of production planning

	2. Determining Production Capacity & Timelines

		Demand alone is not sufficient; capacity constraints matter
		Example:

		Demand = 100 vehicles/month
		Capacity = 30 vehicles/month

		Production planning focuses on what is realistically achievable
		Emphasis is on:

		Optimal capacity utilization
		High quality
		Lower costs

	3. Evaluating Raw Materials

		Raw materials are the backbone of production
		If even one critical material is unavailable, production must be:

		Replanned
		Optimized
		Delayed if needed

		Cost fluctuations of raw materials also influence planning decisions


	4. Production Control, Quality Control & Accounting

		Quantity without quality is meaningless
		Documentation, compliance, and traceability are critical
		Production cannot scale unless:

		Quality standards are met
		Costs are tracked
		Processes are controlled

	5. Evaluation & Continuous Improvement

		Production planning is not a one-time activity
		It must adapt to:

		Changing demand
		Consumer behavior
		New SKUs
		Seasonality
		
		Machine maintenance and system improvements are an integral part of planning

	6. Completion of Finished Goods

		After manufacturing:

		Final quality checks are performed
		Packaging (including artwork) is completed

		This step is critical and must be included in planning, not treated as an afterthought
